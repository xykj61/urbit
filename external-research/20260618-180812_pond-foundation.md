## A Sandbox Is TAME, Made Into a Wall

A sandbox is, at heart, our own principles turned into a security boundary. An allow-list is the *positive space* named and asserted — *these* paths, and no others. A resource cap is a bound, stated plainly. A denial that explains itself is *say-why* at the edge of trust. So the decision that Rye is its own language, safe by construction, makes Rye the right substrate for Pond: the discipline that keeps our code honest is the very discipline that keeps an enclosure trustworthy.

The instinct shows already in the smallest place. The first Rishi keeps a *bounded* environment — a clear edge, rather than memory eaten without end. That same bounding, grown up and given walls, is Pond: water held kindly within clear edges, where work flourishes in safety.

## The Primitives Are Our Own: Caravan and Tally

Here is the turn that draws the whole family in. Pond re-grows over the kernel's isolation primitives — namespaces that scope what a process sees, filesystem rules that scope what it touches, resource limits that scope how much it may take. In a borrowed world those primitives belong to someone else's kernel. In *our* world, we are growing the kernel ourselves, and the allocator beneath it, so the primitives Pond rests on are ours to shape:

- **Caravan is the isolation and supervision.** The kernel and process supervisor of our ecosystem, in the lineage of small, correct, chain-loaded service trees, Caravan is where namespaces, filesystem scoping, and the bringing-up and watching-over of processes live. Pond's "what may be seen, what may be changed" is Caravan's isolation surface, focused on a single agent.
- **Tally is the bounded living space.** The garden allocator — regions with clear edges, cleared whole rather than leaked piecemeal — Tally is where bounded memory comes from. Pond's *mem_mb*, its bounded room, is a Tally garden: the metaphor and the mechanism are the same, an enclosure with edges you can point to.

So Pond is not a thing apart. It is Caravan's isolation and Tally's bounds, composed into one wall and handed a single agent to keep. The three share one foundation of bounded, isolated, supervised resource — and the family serves that whole foundation at once.

## How the Family Serves Caravan

- **Rye** is Caravan's substrate. A kernel asks for exactly what TAME gives: explicit control flow, bounds on everything, assertions where a wrong step would be catastrophic, no hidden machinery. And Rye treats RISC-V as solid ground, so Caravan reasons about an open instruction set it can read and own, rather than a black box.
- **Tablecloth** describes what Caravan brings up. In the lineage we studied, one language *declares* the services and their order while the supervisor *realizes* them. Tablecloth is that declaration — what the system is, named by content — and Caravan is the hand that makes it so.
- **The orchestration language** carries Caravan's startup. The bringing-up of services wants surprise-free, interpreter-less sequencing — the execline-spirit member of the family — so that the code which runs when nothing else is there to catch it does the same thing every time.
- **Rishi** is the human's way in. A person at the prompt invokes Caravan, inspects a running tree, composes a launch — terse for the hand, typed underneath. Pond itself becomes something Caravan supervises and Rishi opens.
- **Mantra** gives Caravan a memory. The system's configuration and its succession of states — which services, which versions, which generation — live as a content-addressed weave, so a running system is reproducible from its history rather than from a remembered incantation. The seam between *what the system is* and *what it was* closes, the same way it closes between source and build.

## How the Family Serves Tally

- **Rye** is Tally made principle-first. TAME asks us to bound every allocation and, where we can, to take our memory at the start and clear it whole — which is precisely the garden allocator's shape. The first `rye` tool already allocates from a single process garden the runtime clears on exit; Tally is that pattern grown into a module, with its edges asserted.
- **Tally is the bound that Pond and Caravan both lean on.** Pond's bounded living space and Caravan's per-service memory are the same primitive seen twice: a garden with a stated size. Give that primitive one good home, and every enclosure and every service inherits a memory it cannot silently outgrow.
- **Tablecloth** can budget the gardens, declaring how much room a service or an enclosure may hold; **Rishi** can show them, a `tally` view of which garden holds what; and the **gates** can harden the allocator's invariants, asserting that a region's edges hold and that nothing is handed back twice.

## The Gates Harden the Shared Floor

Pond, Caravan, and Tally all stand on the same standard-library surface — the calls that scope a process, the arithmetic that bounds a region — and the strengthening machinery is built to harden exactly that. One honest nuance carries over from the crypto work: much of this surface is *effectful*, where the parity gate (which shines on pure, deterministic functions) helps less directly, so the weight there shifts to assertions and the additive gate, with careful review. The *pure* parts — policy parsing, path canonicalization, the resource math — are squarely parity-testable, and they join the witnesses so we harden them the proven way.

---

## One Value Model, Seam-Free

A Pond policy, a Caravan service description, a Tally budget — each is a *value*, not a scatter of stringly flags. Because the family shares one value model, a policy composed in Tablecloth flows through a Rishi pipeline into Pond, Caravan, or Tally with nothing flattened to text and reparsed — no marshaling seam, exactly where a security boundary can least afford ambiguity. And Mantra can version any of them, so an enclosure's policy or a system's generation has an enduring, content-addressed name.

## The Enclosure as a Value

This is the shape, sketched in the Rishi we are growing toward (`../rishi/examples/pond.rish`; today's Rishi runs only the small `hello.rish`):

```
let here = cwd()

let enclosure = {
    read:   ["/usr" "/lib" "/etc/ssl/certs"],   # Caravan's isolation: what it sees
    write:  [here],                              # Caravan's isolation: what it shapes
    net:    false,                               # Caravan's isolation: the wall
    mem_mb: 4096,                                # Tally's garden: a bounded room
    procs:  512,                                 # a bounded liveliness
}

assert (enclosure.write contains here)  else  "the project must be writable"
assert (enclosure.net == false)         else  "this enclosure stays offline"

# Caravan's isolation and Tally's bounds, composed into one wall.
pond open enclosure -- ./squashfs-root/AppRun --no-sandbox "${here}"
```

The allow-lists are Caravan's isolation, named and asserted. The size bounds are Tally's gardens, stated plainly. Rishi composes them into one value and opens the gate. Each line is a permission a person can read aloud, weigh, and trust.

## Caveats

We name what is still ahead, plainly. Pond, Caravan, and Tally are largely designs and seeds today; the enclosure we actually work inside remains the borrowed one, and the Rishi that would run `pond.rish` is still growing toward lists and records. A security boundary asks for more than our gates can give alone — real-world testing, adversarial review, and time. And the effectful surface beneath all three leans more on stated invariants than on differential parity. We hold the vision gently, and we build toward it one proven step at a time.

## The Path

Near: the borrowed enclosure keeps us safe while we build. Middle: strengthen the pure standard-library surface that Pond, Caravan, and Tally will share; grow Rishi toward the policy-as-value of `pond.rish`; and carry Caravan and Tally from research into `active-designing` as decided modules. Far: Pond open over Caravan's isolation and Tally's gardens, every policy a value, every generation remembered by Mantra — the kernel, the allocator, and the enclosure, all grown in our own hand and all of one grain.
