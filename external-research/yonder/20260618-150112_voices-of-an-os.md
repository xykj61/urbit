# 989 · One Language or More? Rye, the Shell, and the Voices of an OS

*Should our future OS speak in one language or more? Should Rye also be its shell and scripting language, or should the shell have its own — a more TAME execline, or something in the spirit of Zig, Haskell, or Nix/Tablecloth? And where does Mantra, our version control, sit among them? The question rests on a hidden assumption: that "the shell" is one thing. It is not. An OS speaks several languages, held together by one memory, and the art is to make them a coherent family rather than one language stretched thin or a babel of strangers.*

## The Question

We are building Rye as a systems language, and an operating system, Caravan, to run on it. A natural thrift suggests itself: if we already have Rye, why design a second language for the shell? Let the language that writes the programs also write the scripts. Against that thrift stands an old instinct from Unix and Plan 9 — that the shell wants a language of its own — and the question of which spirit that language should carry: the no-surprise chain-loading of execline, the explicit comptime of Zig, the typed composition of Haskell, or the declarative laziness of Nix and our own Tablecloth. And once a family of them exists, a further question presses: where does Mantra, our version control, sit among them?

One language, or more? We will answer it — yet first we must take the question apart, because it hides a false premise.

---

## "The Shell" Is Not One Thing

When we say *shell scripting*, we blur together at least four distinct jobs, each with its own master:

1. **Programs** — the long-lived, performance-bearing code that does the real work. Its master is control: explicit memory, precise types, speed, safety under load.
2. **Configuration and build** — the description of *what the system is*: which packages, which services, which versions, reproducibly. Its master is declarative truth: say what should hold, let the machine realize it, name everything by content.
3. **Orchestration** — bringing the system *up*: starting processes in the right order, supervising them, wiring their inputs and outputs. Its master is predictable sequencing with no surprises, because this is the code that runs when nothing else is there to catch it.
4. **Interactive glue** — a human at a prompt, composing flows: list these, filter those, pipe the result onward. Its master is terseness and immediacy — short to type, quick to answer, forgiving to explore.

And threaded through all four, a fifth concern — less a job done at a prompt than a dimension beneath them all: **memory** — the history of every program, configuration, script, and service as it changes across time. That is Mantra's domain, and we place it once the four are settled.

A single language asked to serve all four masters at once must betray most of them. This is why the question "Rye, or a shell language?" cannot be answered yes or no: it is really four questions wearing one coat — with a fifth, memory, woven beneath.

---

## The Honest Pull of One Language

Before we divide, let us be fair to unification, because its pull is real and principled.

If everything is Rye, there is *one mental model* to learn, *one toolchain* to keep, and — most deeply — *no seam*. A system, in Rich Hickey's framing, is an ensemble of programs flowing values to one another, and every seam between languages is a place where values must be flattened to text and reparsed, losing their types and their guarantees in the crossing. If a script and a program speak the same values, that loss never happens; a pipeline carries living, typed values, not reconstituted strings. And a script written in Rye is a *checkable program* — it gets the same assertions, the same types, the same strengthening as everything else. The Lisp machines lived this way; Urbit lives this way now, one language from the metal to the prompt. The dream is coherent, and it deserves our respect.

So we keep its best prize — *no seam* — and look for a way to claim it without paying its price.

---

## Why One Language Still Fails the Fit

The price is fit. A systems language and an interactive shell optimize for opposite things, and the opposition is not incidental — it is the whole point of each.

Rye, in the spirit of Zig, is explicit, ahead-of-time compiled, and verbose precisely where verbosity buys control and safety. That is exactly right for a program and exactly wrong for a prompt: no one wants a compile step between typing a command and seeing its answer, nor the ceremony of a `main` and a return type to list a directory. Stretch Rye to be terse and interactive and you blunt the explicitness that makes it safe; stretch the shell to be a full systems language and you drown its immediacy in ceremony. Every attempt in computing's history to make the systems language be the shell, or the shell be the systems language, has produced something that serves one master poorly and the other worse. TAME asks for simplicity and for the right nouns and verbs; both point to tools shaped to their work, not one tool hammered flat to cover all of it.

The resolution, then: **a coherent family — rather than one language stretched thin, rather than a lone separate shell — each member fit to its master, all sharing one value model so the seam never opens.** Coherence over uniformity.

---

## Weighing the Spirits, Job by Job

With the four jobs named, the spirits the question offered each find their rightful home — and a couple find that they do not belong in the shell at all.

- **The Zig spirit** — explicit, comptime, low-level — is *Rye's own* spirit, and it belongs to the **programs**. It is the wrong spirit for glue: too low to the metal, too long in the saying. We keep it where it earns its keep, and carry it no further.

- **The Nix/Tablecloth spirit** — declarative, lazy, content-addressed — is exactly right for **configuration and build**, which is precisely why we already have **Tablecloth**. You describe what the system should be and let realization follow; laziness serves you, computing only what is demanded. Yet that same laziness is a poor fit for a prompt, where a human means *do this now, in this order* — a command typed is a command expected to run, rather than a thunk deferred until forced. Tablecloth for the system's shape; not for the human's hands.

- **The execline spirit** — interpreter-less chain-loading, bounded, no security surprises — is the right spirit for **orchestration**, the bringing-up of services that is Caravan's lineage. The instinct for "a more TAME execline" lands exactly here. This layer wants no resident interpreter, no parsing ambiguity, no cleverness — just a predictable sequence of process hand-offs that does the same thing every time. It is the wrong spirit for interactive use — its syntax is alien to a human exploring — yet the right one for the init that must never surprise.

- **The Haskell spirit** — typed values and composition — is the right *core* for **interactive glue**, because a pipeline truly *is* function composition: `a | b | c` is `c` after `b` after `a` over a stream of values. Strong types make that composition safe; structured values make it lossless. Yet Haskell's other halves — purity and laziness — must be set down here, because a shell's whole vocation is *effects in order*: spawn, write, move, now. So we borrow Haskell's typed composition and decline its purity, which is, fittingly, exactly the bargain a structured-data shell already proves can work.

No single spirit fits "the shell," because the shell is four jobs. Each spirit fits one of them.

---

## The Fifth Voice: Mantra, the Memory

Mantra is the member of the family you do not *write in* the way you write the others. The four above are synchronic — they say what the system does now. Mantra is diachronic: it remembers what they all said, across every change, forever. It is less a language than a weave, a content-addressed structure that holds the whole history of a thing in its own shape.

And here the shared value model pays its deepest dividend. Because a Rye program, a Tablecloth configuration, an orchestration script, and a value at the prompt are all *the same kind of value*, Mantra can version them with a single mechanism: one weave, content-addressed, holding the history of values rather than the history of text. The program and its config and the service that runs it are not three histories in three formats stitched together — they are one history, in one grain. This is exactly the seam `20260617-195312_mantra.md` set out to close: the gap between a source's history and the build made from it disappears, because both are the same content-addressed values in the same weave. Memory, for the whole family, named in one grain of hashes.

So Mantra is not a fifth syntax to learn. It is the family's shared past — the reason the other four can change freely and still be trusted, because nothing they produce is ever truly lost.

---

## The Recommendation: A Family With One Value Model

We recommend a small family of TAME-coherent languages, sharing one value model, one safety discipline, and one grammar-feel — so that crossing between them costs nothing and learning one eases the next:

- **Rye** — the **programs**. Systems work, in the Zig spirit, strengthened our way.
- **Tablecloth** — the **configuration and build**. Declarative and content-addressed, in the Nix spirit, already ours.
- **An orchestration language** — the **services and init**, in the execline spirit: no resident interpreter, bounded, surprise-free. This may need no separate language at all — Tablecloth could *describe* the service graph and Caravan *execute* it in the chain-loading manner — yet if it wants its own small grammar, that grammar is a TAME execline.
- **An interactive shell** — the **human glue**: terse, typed, composition-first, and effect-honest, a sibling of Rye rather than Rye itself. We name it **Rishi** — a seer who hears the human and speaks faithfully to the machine, carrying typed values from one command into the next; the name keeps bhakti's devotion and a sattvic, plant-kind gentleness, true to our vow. We take it up in full in [`../active-designing/yonder/20260619-051312_the-faithful-hand.md`](../active-designing/yonder/20260619-051312_the-faithful-hand.md) and [`../rishi/README.md`](../rishi/README.md).
- **Mantra** — the **memory**. Less a language you compose in than the content-addressed weave that remembers all the others, versioning programs, configs, scripts, and services alike — because they share one value model, their history is one weave.

The move that earns the one-language prize without its price is this: **all of these share a single value model.** A value produced by a Rye program, carried through a Rishi pipeline, stored by a Tablecloth build, and remembered by a Mantra weave is *the same value*, not four flattened-and-reparsed copies of it. The seam Hickey warns against never opens, because there is nothing to marshal across — not between the languages, and not between the present and the past. We get coherence — one ecosystem, one set of guarantees — without the false economy of one syntax pretending to fit every hand.

---

## The Answer, Plainly

So: **Rye should not be the shell scripting language**, and the shell should not be a single separate language either. Rye is the systems language; the OS speaks a coherent family beside it — Tablecloth for what the system is, an execline-spirit language for bringing it up, a typed, composition-first shell (Rishi) for the human at the prompt, and Mantra as the memory that remembers them all. They are siblings, not strangers: one value model, one safety, one feel. That is how an operating system can speak several languages and still sound like one voice.
