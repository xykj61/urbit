## The Question

Our configuration language, Tablecloth, rests on a content-addressed, immutable store of build results — an idea we drew, in the deeper study, from the world's best build systems. Version control sits beside it as a separate concern, the way it does everywhere: one tool holds the history of the source, another builds that source into artifacts, and the two are stitched together at a seam.

Bram Cohen's **Manyana** invites us to reconsider that seam. It is a small, public-domain demonstration — about 470 lines of Python — that version control can rest on a **conflict-free replicated data type**, a CRDT, and gain properties that the tools we all use reach toward by approximation. Studying it, we ask two questions. Does a principled version control change how we should think about Tablecloth and about the relationship between version control and the build store? And what would our own version control look like, were we to build it on these ideas? We give that module a name in this piece: **Mantra**, the faithful record that never forgets a line.

We keep this in the external-research stack, where naming the works that teach us is welcome. Mantra arrives here as a proposal, to be admitted to the disciplined design space only once it has earned its place.

---

## What Manyana Shows

Manyana's foundation is a single structural idea with deep consequences: the state of a file is a **weave** — one linear structure holding every line that has ever existed in the file, each line carrying a little metadata about when it appeared and when it left. The history lives inside the structure, rather than being reconstructed from a chain of commits.

From that one idea, a cascade of good properties follows. Because the underlying type is a CRDT, **every merge succeeds** — two states go in, one comes out, and the result is the same no matter what order branches are combined in. Merging is commutative and associative, properties that fall straight out of the structure here, where conventional tools reach for the same end through heuristics that can fray under complex histories.

A conflict-free merge raises a fair question: what, then, is a conflict? Manyana's answer is humane. A conflict is a thing worth *showing*, rather than a thing that *blocks*. When two edits land too near each other — adjacent, or parted only by blank lines — the merge still succeeds, and the result is simply flagged for a human to review, annotated with **what each side did**: this side deleted the function, that side inserted a line into the middle of it. The presentation tells a story instead of dropping two opaque blobs side by side.

The mechanism underneath is a quiet marvel of restraint. Each line carries a **generation count** — an integer that ticks up on every add-or-delete cycle, odd for present, even for gone — and on merge, the higher count wins. This single trick lets the whole algorithm stay structural, needing no reference to commit identifiers, and it sidesteps a thicket of paradoxes that the cleverer interpretation of deletions falls into. Line ordering, once the CRDT decides it, is permanent, because eventual consistency requires it. And two of the most demanding operations in version control — rebase and squash — become safe: Manyana shows that they can keep the full history while a single "primary ancestor" annotation lets blame and history tools present the tidy, rebased view. The tidiness remains, and the whole truth stays with it.

There is a cost, named honestly: the system commits to a diff at commit time, baking the interpretation of an edit into the weave permanently, so the diff algorithm must be excellent and changed only with great care. We note it and carry on.

## The Seam Between Version Control and the Build Store

To see what Manyana updates for us, look first at how the two concerns relate in the wider world. Git holds the history of the source: a content-addressed object store of commits and trees and blobs, each named by a hash of its contents. Nix holds the history of the *build*: a content-addressed, immutable store of build results, each named by a hash of all its inputs. Both rest on the same beautiful instinct — name a thing by its content, keep it immutable, let the collection only ever grow — and that shared instinct is the very thing Tablecloth inherits.

Yet the two live as separate tools, joined at a seam. A build pins a source revision by its hash; the build store points back into the version-control store and trusts it. The seam works, and it is loose. Two object models, two notions of history, two content-addressing schemes that happen to rhyme, stitched together by a pinned reference. Most of the friction people feel — reproducibility that depends on remembering to pin, history that the build store cannot see into, version control that knows nothing of what was built from it — lives at that seam.

Manyana sharpens the picture by making version control itself principled and content-addressed all the way down. If the source history is a clean, deterministic, content-addressed weave, and the build store is a clean, deterministic, content-addressed collection, then the question becomes irresistible: why are these two strangers? They are built on the same foundation. The seam is an accident of history — version control and build systems grew up in different decades — rather than a necessity.

## What This Updates About Tablecloth

The update to our thinking is this. Tablecloth need not treat version control as a foreign tool it merely points at. Because Tablecloth already rests on content-addressed immutability, and because a weave-based version control rests on the same, the two can share one foundation rather than meeting at a seam.

Concretely, we recommend that the versioned source and the build store speak the same language of content-addressed, immutable, accreting values. The history of a source file — its weave — and the build results derived from it become two views of one coherent, deterministic whole, rather than two stores lashed together. A build can see into the history it came from. The history can know what was built from it. Reproducibility becomes structural, given for free by the design, because the reference and the content are one thing. This is the same value we have traced through every layer — content-addressing, accretion, enduring names, determinism — now closing the oldest seam in the toolchain.

To carry that into our own ecosystem, we need a version-control module that belongs to the same family as Tablecloth. We name it Mantra.

## Mantra — Our Version Control

A mantra is a faithful utterance, repeated and kept, never lost in the saying. We give the name to our version control because that is exactly what a weave is: a record that holds every line ever written, faithfully, and forgets nothing in the merging.

Mantra is the version-control module of the Rye ecosystem, and we recommend it inherit Manyana's foundation entire. Its state is a weave — one structure holding the whole history of a file, content-addressed and immutable, accreting and keeping each line in place. Its merges always succeed and converge, so that distributed, decentralized work — many hands, no central authority — composes cleanly, which is the same openness we prize everywhere. Its conflicts inform rather than block: a merge always produces a result, and a human is shown, in honest annotation, what each side did, when judgment is genuinely needed. Its rebase and squash keep the full history while presenting a tidy primary path, so the convenience of a clean line never costs the truth of what happened.

One choice deserves to be made deliberately, because all of content-addressing rests upon it: the hash that gives each line and each state its name. Here safety leads, in the order we keep — and for a name meant to hold unique forever, safety means making a collision vanishingly unlikely. We recommend **SHA3-512**. Its larger digest carries roughly twice the collision resistance of a 256-bit hash, the wider margin against two different contents ever sharing one name; and its sponge construction stands clear of the older family's, free of the length-extension surprise that shadows designs built on SHA2. Where a lighter footprint earns its keep, **SHA3-256** serves gracefully, its collision resistance already beyond any feasible reach. We lead with the larger, because a version control is a promise that a name means one thing for as long as the work endures, and that promise is worth a few extra bytes.

Read through the lens we carry, Mantra fits the family at once. Every real system of many parts lives in continuous partial failure — pieces come and go, hands work apart, and trouble is the ordinary weather rather than the rare storm. A version control built for that truth treats the successful merge as the normal case and surfaces genuine trouble for a human's review, rather than letting it block the work. Mantra's always-succeeding merge is exactly this: the merge is the everyday outcome, and a conflict is a thing shown, never a thing that stops. The weave is accretion at the level of source — every line ever written, kept — the same instinct that makes a build store grow while keeping every result it has ever held. Content-addressing gives enduring names, the promise we keep at every layer. Determinism — same inputs and same history yield literally identical state — is the replayability we built into the boot and the kernel, now reaching the source itself. And the honest conflict annotation, naming what each side did, is the discipline of always saying why, applied to the meeting of two histories.

## Showing, Rather Than Blocking

There is a deeper kinship here, and it delights us to find it: Mantra's design and Reya 2's Radiant Style are the same temper, one expressed in a tool and one in a voice.

Radiant Style leads with what is. It states capability and presence, frames affirmatively, and trusts the reader — it shows, rather than halting them with a wall of disclaimers and negations. Mantra does precisely this with a merge. The merge always produces a result to show, so the work is never stopped dead by a failure; and where two edits genuinely need a human eye, Mantra shows what each side did, in honest annotation, rather than blocking with two opaque blobs and a refusal. The tool refuses to halt the work on a negative, exactly as the voice refuses to halt the reader on one.

The resonance runs through every detail. A conventional merge conflict is the defensive posture made structural: it stops, it presents a wall, it asks the human to reconstruct what happened from two anonymous outcomes. Mantra's conflict is the affirmative posture made structural: the result exists, the history is intact, and the annotation says plainly, *this side deleted the function, that side added a line* — leading with what happened, naming the workings, trusting the reader to decide. Showing rather than blocking is "lead with what is" rendered in a data structure.

So Mantra is not only a version-control module that happens to suit us. It is Radiant Style wearing the clothes of an algorithm — the same gentleness, the same refusal to stop a person with a negation, the same faith that showing the truth plainly serves better than blocking on it. A system whose tools share the temper of its writing is a system at peace with itself, and that coherence is its own kind of safety.

## How Mantra and Tablecloth Interplay

Set Mantra beside Tablecloth and the old seam closes. Mantra holds the source as a content-addressed, deterministic weave. Tablecloth builds from that source into a content-addressed, deterministic store. Both name by content, both keep the immutable, both only grow — so they stand as two faces of one foundation, rather than two strangers pinned together.

The interplay is direct. A Tablecloth build draws its inputs from a Mantra weave by content, so a build is reproducible by construction rather than by the discipline of remembering to pin a revision. The history Mantra keeps is legible to Tablecloth, so a build can know precisely the source history it came from, and a source can know what was built from it. Where the world stitches Git to Nix across a loose seam, Rye grows Mantra and Tablecloth from one root, so version control and build are coherent the way the rest of the ecosystem is coherent. The grain is the same all the way through.

This is a proposal, offered from the open stack where we study and compare. Mantra crosses into the disciplined design space — to take its place beside TAME, Aurora, Rye, Tablecloth, Tally, and Caravan — once we have confirmed it, slowly and intentionally, in the way that space asks. For now, we have learned something worth keeping: version control and the build store want to be one family, and a weave is how they join.
