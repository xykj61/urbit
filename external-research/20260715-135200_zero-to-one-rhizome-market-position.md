## Why This Doc Exists

An Acme Corporation employee reading the roadmap so far sees a growing family of named vanes — Glow, Pool, Quin, Rhyz, Ojjo, Neth — landing lap by lap, each proven by a runnable witness before it is called true. That is the *what*. This doc answers a different question: *where is this headed, and what is it, in market terms, relative to Urbit's own network and the wider concentric circles around it* — the built-system teachers in `gratitude/`, the Azimuth economy this fork's own identity already sits inside, the broader decentralized-compute and personal-server world Urbit occupies one corner of.

The frame borrowed here is Peter Thiel's *Zero to One* — thanked as a source of vocabulary and a way of asking the question, not quoted, and every claim below re-expressed in this tree's own terms rather than Thiel's. The doc's second half resolves a real tension: Thiel's own monopoly-seeking logic assumes a single dominant vertical (one trunk, one root), while this fork's own chosen identity concept — **Rhyz**, reviving **Kumara**, a rhizome-named identity model — assumes no single root at all. That tension is worked through honestly below, not talked around.

## Where This Is Headed, Concretely

Grounded in what already stands, not speculation:

- **Glow** — the fused Rye-Hoon language, four kernel variants (**Riyo**/**Reya**, **Trey**/**Triz** — two deliberately diverse-redundant pairs) named and scoped, none yet running its first program.
- **Neth** — a TigerBeetle-shaped settlement vane over signed facts. Its own build ladder closed a full arc this season: a deterministic-root witness, a small serial core, a two-posture root install (trust the issuing source outright, verify a peer's repair by hash-chain), apply-step crash safety (written is not visible until admitted), a deterministic fault-injection simulation, and — the newest lap — a real Comlink-carried hop across an actual socket, proving a signed record folds identically whether it arrives in-process or over a real wire.
- **Mantra** — the referential namespace (`peer/bolt/revision/path`, recall returns the same bytes for all time) gained the same apply-step discipline as its own sibling module, composed onto its existing foundation without editing it.
- **Pool**, **Quin**, **Rhyz**, **Ojjo** — named, scoped, none yet built. Pool as a content-and-data market (Gall's own application role, generalized); Quin as an inference vane unifying four existing voices; Rhyz reviving Azimuth's own point/planet/star identity words directly; Ojjo as a benchmarking vane proving Hoon/Dojo vs. Glow/Rish parity with real timed stats, once Glow can run something to time.

**Near-term milestones**, honestly ordered: Glow's first running program (the trigger Ojjo's own first lap waits on), Quin hosting its first real agent, and Neth's fault-injection simulation combined with its own now-real carriage lap (faults over an actual wire, not an in-process stand-in — named as open, not yet attempted). **Longer horizon**: the four kernel variants actually diverging in measured practice, Pool's flagship market lap, and a gated commit-message reframe that would make the "proposal, not fork" framing visible in the git history itself.

None of this displaces Urbit's own network. Every naming decision in this tree carries the same explicit caveat: proposal only, nothing renamed or seated until it earns its own lap and, where it touches the wider Urbit world, until Urbit's own maintainers have a word.

## Who This Is, in Market Terms — the Honest Version

Urbit already holds a real, if modest, monopoly position in Thiel's own sense: the only serious, decade-old attempt at a sovereign personal-server operating system with genuine on-chain identity ownership — 256 real galaxy points, real stars and planets trading as ERC-721 assets, a small yet technically fluent community that persists. Competing head-on for that network — building a rival stack from nothing and hoping to draw users away from an entrenched incumbent with a decade of continuity behind it — is precisely the trap Thiel names as "1 to n": entering an already-small market against a network-effect incumbent, rather than creating anything new. Named plainly, without flattering the alternative: that would be a losing move at this scale, and this fork's own explicit reframe away from "competitive fork" and toward "collaborative proposal" is the strategically correct instinct, whether or not it was reasoned in these exact market terms when it was chosen.

The genuine "secret" worth having here is not a rival network. It is a **discipline**: witness-before-truth. Every claim in this tree is either a runnable, falsifiable proof or explicitly marked unproven (Two Rooms, "designed-not-built"). Hoon and Arvo carry a famously steep trust barrier for newcomers — sparse working examples, few mechanically-checked correctness claims outside the kernel's own core. A rigorously witnessed, TAME-guided implementation lineage that proves its own claims mechanically, lap by lap, is a real and defensible niche *inside* that world rather than a replacement *for* it. The Neth ladder closed this same season is a small, concrete instance: a claim (*"Comlink carriage just works"*) turned out false the moment it was actually run, against a real 340-byte wire ceiling versus a 500-byte log line, and the fix was proven byte-for-byte rather than asserted in prose.

So the market role is not "found the next Urbit." It is closer to a specialized verification-and-safety practice operating inside an existing sovereign-compute ecosystem, whose product is *proof* rather than *users*. Thiel's own advice is explicit that a monopoly should start small and well-defined before it expands — "the most rigorously witnessed correctness layer for Urbit-adjacent settlement, identity, and inference code" is a genuinely small niche, and, as far as this study can tell, one nobody else is presently trying to own.

## Resolving Zero to One With Rhizome Realism

Here is the actual tension, named without smoothing it over: Thiel's monopoly logic is **arborescent** — one trunk, one root, a single vertical stack owned end to end. A rhizome — the concept this fork's own **Rhyz**/**Kumara** identity module is named for — has no single root, no center, many entry and exit points, and grows by lateral connection rather than vertical conquest. Choosing a rhizome as your own identity's namesake while reading a monopoly-theory book is not a contradiction needing an excuse. It is two different claims about **where** dominance is sought.

The resolution: **do not seek a monopoly over the network. Seek a monopoly over one function within it.** A rhizome has no dominant root, and yet any single node inside one can still be the only node performing one particular function well enough that the rest of the mesh comes to depend on it. This is not a compromise splitting the difference between the two ideas — it is Thiel's own "small niche first" counsel, relocated onto rhizome terrain instead of tree terrain. The move is not to out-compete Urbit for its network. It is to become, within Urbit's own wider mesh — alongside every teacher already held in `gratitude/` with attribution rather than appropriation (IronBeetle, Nix, s6/skalibs, Genode/seL4, GNU Taler, and the rest) — the node that owns **verified correctness** as its one irreplaceable specialty. A rhizome's currency is density of connection; being the node nothing else can be routed around is still a monopoly, simply measured in indispensability rather than acreage.

Concretely, the day's own Neth work is a small instance of exactly this move: not "build a rival settlement system" but "prove, byte-for-byte, that this one piece works" — and let that proof, not a user count, be the thing worth connecting to.
