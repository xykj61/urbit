## In One Breath

An **aspect** is a named capability that lives *inside* another module's home — sharing that host's budget and witnesses, owning no directory of its own.

A **module** is a named home: its own directory, invariants, witnesses, and a meaning a person can say in one line.

**Graduation** happens on the day a **second consumer from outside the host** starts depending on that aspect's public surface. Until then, the aspect stays nested. The tree grows doors when two hands outside knock — not when elegance alone asks for a rename.

---

## The Everyday Picture

Think of a workshop. One craftsperson keeps a sharp tool on their own bench. That tool is an **aspect** of their shop. The day a neighbor starts borrowing it for real work — and a second neighbor does too — the tool earns a **shared shelf** with its own label. That shelf is the **module**.

One borrower still means "well-organized drawer." Two outside borrowers mean "shared infrastructure." Gall's Law agrees: working wholes grow from smaller wholes that already worked.

---

## What Counts as a Consumer

| Counts | Does not count |
|--------|----------------|
| Another module imports the aspect's public API | The host using its own submodule |
| A Pond app above the modules depends on it at build or run time | Tests inside the host that exercise the aspect |

Ask: *If the host vanished, would this other module still need this capability under its own name?* Two yes answers from distinct hosts mean graduation is due.

## What the Rule Protects

- **Aspects stay honest** while Gall's Law still favors small wholes.
- **Lexicon names may lead** implementation; directories follow consumers.
- **No lint replaces judgment** — graduation is architectural, witnessed in prose and parity, not a textual scan.

When the second consumer lands, the checklist is mechanical: create `module/`, move source, publish a minimal surface, update imports, wire witnesses, refresh LEXICON, session-log the stamp and parity count.
