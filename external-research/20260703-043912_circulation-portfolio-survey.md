# The Circulation Portfolio — A Survey of Public Companies Whose Work Runs on Public Money and Wages

*Kaeden asked which public companies, as of July 2026, most nearly embody the pattern our
foundations describe: employment funded by the currency issuer, doing work communities named as
valuable, in industries that can run for decades on circulation rather than extraction. This
survey answers as research for understanding. It builds a frame from our own writings, verifies
the policy ground with fresh July 2026 sources, walks the archetypes with their honest caveats,
and ends on the finding that matters most: the chapters closest to our heart are the ones the
public markets have barely written — which is the strongest argument in this whole document for

## Part One — The Frame: Five Lenses

The foundations writings supply the lenses; this survey only grinds them to fit the stock pages.

**Public share.** How much of the company's revenue is the issuer's spending, one step removed —
appropriations, formula funds, reimbursements, regulated-utility capital plans? The purer the
share, the more nearly the company is what the aspiring-radiance letter calls sovereign
investment wearing a private payroll.

**Wages as the product.** What fraction of each revenue dollar becomes a worker's paycheck
before anything else? A caregiver agency and a craft contractor pass most of the dollar straight
to hands; a royalty extractor passes almost none. This is the "fiat employment" test in its
plainest form: the public mark enters, and its first landing is a wage.

**Durability of the authorization.** Public money arrives on a ladder of firmness. At the top,
permanent statute and entitlement reimbursement. Next, multi-year contract authority from a
trust fund. Then multi-year appropriations that an administration merely administers. Then
annual appropriations. At the bottom, discretionary grants an administration can reshape or
pause. The July 2026 ground below shows every rung of this ladder moving at once.

**Circulation geometry.** Our money-close-to-home essay names the test: does the dollar turn
where it landed — wages spent locally, materials sourced regionally, value returning each pass —
or does it drain toward a distant headquarters or out of the living world entirely through
depletion? Optimize for the torus, never the siphon.

**The ahimsa screen.** Some of the largest publicly traded recipients of issuer spending fail
the first value outright and leave this survey before it starts: weapons makers, animal
agriculture and its processors, fossil extraction, and carceral operators. Naming the exclusion
plainly matters, because by raw headcount the excluded firms are among the biggest
"government-funded employers" in the market — and the whole point of this frame is that raw
extraction power is exactly what we decline to optimize for.

## Part Two — The July 2026 Ground, Verified

Each thread below was checked against fresh sources on this bench tonight rather than carried
from the sibling survey on trust.

**The surface-transportation season.** The current infrastructure law's highway, transit, and
rail authorities expire September 30, 2026. The House committee approved a five-year, roughly
$580 billion successor — the BUILD America 250 Act — by a 62-to-2 vote on May 22, 2026, with
about $474 billion as guaranteed Highway Trust Fund contract authority; the Senate has yet to
move, and observers on all sides expect a near-term extension of existing authorities if
negotiations run into the fall, as they usually do. Two honest notes ride with the headline: the
guaranteed share tilts further toward highway formulas, and analysts read the bill as reducing
relative transit spending and ending guaranteed passenger-rail funding — a durability downgrade
for the transit-heavy end of the sector even as the overall number grows.

**The clean-electricity credit cliff — tomorrow.** Under the July 2025 tax law, wind and solar
projects keep their technology-neutral credits only if construction begins by July 4, 2026;
projects starting later must be fully in service by December 31, 2027. A court decision restored
the five-percent-spend path alongside the physical-work test, but the date itself stands, and it
is tomorrow. Storage, geothermal, hydro, and nuclear keep full credits for starts through 2033 —
so the durability line inside "clean energy" now runs straight between wind-and-solar developers
on one side and storage, grid, and firm-clean builders on the other.

**The care-coverage tightening.** The same 2025 law conditions coverage for the expansion
population on eighty monthly hours of work or qualifying activity, with six-month renewals,
effective January 1, 2027 — several states began earlier in 2026. Federal projections expect
roughly 2.3 million people disenrolled in 2027, rising past three million after; enrollment has
already fallen about 4.8 million, six percent, in the year through March 2026, declining in
every state. The issuer's ledger note beside this survey reads the law here: universal designs
hold, and means-tested, paperwork-gated designs bend, shedding people the program was built for.

**The yellow-bus reset.** On February 19, 2026, the environmental agency overhauled the $5
billion clean-school-bus program: the 2024 rebate round — over $900 million in applications —
was cancelled outright, eligibility widened beyond electric to gas-derived and other fuels, and
a new funding round is promised later in 2026, with roughly $2.3 billion still to award and a
statutory floor keeping at least half of funds on zero-emission buses. The lesson is the
durability ladder in miniature: money appropriated by statute yet administered by grant bends
with each administration.

**The broadband build year.** The $42.5 billion broadband deployment program reaches
construction across dozens of states in 2026, after a rewrite made the program
technology-neutral so satellite and fixed wireless now compete with fiber for awards. The
build is labor-starved by design: the Commerce Department estimates the country needs more than
two hundred thousand additional fiber technicians to keep pace — public money queued up and
waiting for hands, the fiat-employment thesis in the flesh.

**The concentration parable.** One engineering consultancy watched its largest single client —
nearly a tenth of net revenue and a third of its federal work — vanish within months when the
foreign-aid agency was dissolved: virtually all contracts terminated for convenience, a $92
million impairment, over half a billion dollars of expected annual revenue erased. The same firm
then grew straight through the wound — state-and-local revenue up almost twenty-nine percent for
the year, new water and environmental awards from the engineering corps — because the demand for
its people's skills was broader than the client that died. Both halves are the lesson: any
single public payer can vanish by signature, and durable demand lives in the skill, not the
contract.

## Part Three — The Archetypes, With Their Honest Caveats

No ranking follows, because the lenses measure different goods. Each archetype names the shape
of the public money, the shape of the employment, and the caveat a fresh look must weigh.

### The wires crafts — grid and fiber

The purest large-scale expression of "public money queued for skilled hands" in today's market
is the utility-infrastructure contractor. **Quanta Services** ended the first quarter of 2026
with a record $48.5 billion backlog — up from $44 billion at year-end — and raised full-year
revenue guidance to roughly $35 billion, on transmission, renewables, and data-center
interconnection; its workforce is tens of thousands of linemen and craftworkers, and the wage is
the product. Two caveats: the demand mix now leans heavily on private data-center capital
alongside regulated-utility plans, so this is quasi-public circulation rather than pure
appropriation; and the market has noticed — the shares trade at a premium that assumes the
backlog converts flawlessly. **MYR Group** and **MasTec** work the same crafts at smaller and
broader scale respectively. On the fiber side, **Dycom Industries** enters the broadband build
year with a record backlog and double-digit revenue growth, its self-performing crews the
scarce input the technician shortage names; the caveats are the program's slow state-by-state
release and the technology-neutral rewrite, which lets satellite claim awards fiber crews would
have built.

### Water, always water

Water infrastructure sits nearest the top of the durability ladder — revolving funds, rate-based
utility plans, and health mandates that no administration unwinds — and it is where the
concentration-parable firm ran when its aid client vanished. **Xylem** (pumps, treatment,
metering), **Mueller Water Products** (the fittings and hydrants beneath every main), and
**Advanced Drainage Systems** (stormwater, made increasingly from recycled material) supply the
crafts rather than employ them at scale, so they score higher on durability than on
wages-as-the-product. The design-and-build labor lives in the consultancies below and in the
regional contractors the funds hire.

### The design-and-deliver minds

Engineering and program-delivery consultancies are labor almost without remainder — the payroll
is the product — with public shares commonly a third to a half of revenue. **Tetra Tech** is the
parable and the pivot in one ticker: wounded by a client that vanished, first-ranked in water
for a dozen years running, now growing on state, local, and water work. **AECOM**, **Jacobs**,
and **Stantec** carry the same shape across transportation, water, and environment, and each
rides the reauthorization season's outcome. The caveat is the parable itself: government
backlog is terminable for convenience, and the durable asset is the bench of licensed people,
which walks out the door each evening and must be treated well enough to walk back in.

### Retrofit paid by the savings

The energy-savings performance contract is a forty-year-old statutory vehicle by which a public
building hires the retrofit — insulation, controls, heat pumps, solar — and pays from the energy
bill it stops paying, no special appropriation required. **Ameresco** is the archetype: an
operations-and-maintenance backlog above a billion dollars and contracts that run decades. The
caveats are live: federal work is roughly a fifth of its revenue and the current
administration's posture toward efficiency programs has made that fifth murky, even as city and
county demand for the same vehicle rises precisely because federal support is receding. The
mechanism is the CIVIC ideal — the reward is the measured saving — and the near-term climate is
the risk.

### Care — the purest fiat employment on the tape

Home and community care funded by public payers is the closest thing the market offers to a Job
Guarantee in miniature: the revenue is nearly all reimbursement, the cost is nearly all
caregiver wages, and the work is exactly what the foundations name — dignity delivered at home.
**Addus HomeCare** runs about $1.5 billion of revenue, overwhelmingly public-payer, serving
tens of thousands of clients, with recent rate increases in its three anchor states and margins
that have grown with scale. The caveats are the heaviest in this survey: the January 2027
work-reporting rules and six-month renewals arrive against enrollment already falling in every
state; managed-care intermediaries take a growing seat between the issuer and the wage; the
enforcement climate is sharpening; and a rule that would have required eighty percent of these
payments to reach direct-care wages — the reward pointed straight at the good — is now expected
to be eliminated rather than enforced. A CIVIC reader holds both truths: this is the right
work, and the current rulebook is bending away from the workers who do it.

### The yellow bus, reset

**Blue Bird** builds the school buses the five-billion-dollar program was written to replace,
in a unionized Georgia plant, and rode the program's first rounds hard. The February reset
cancelled the pending rebates, widened eligibility to fuels our frame reads as a step sideways,
and paused deliveries tied to federal awards — while the statutory floor still routes at least
half of the remaining $2.3 billion to zero-emission buses, and state and utility programs keep
their own lanes open. The archetype teaches the administered-grant rung of the ladder better
than any other single name.

## Part Four — The Thin Chapters, and What They Argue

Now the finding the whole survey was walked to reach. Hold the foundations' dearest chapters up
to the public markets and the shelves are nearly bare:

| Foundations chapter | Public-market pure plays as of July 2026 |
|---|---|
| Veganic, no-till, regenerative agriculture | None of scale — the work is done by private farms, co-ops, and land trusts |
| Fiber-crop building materials (hemp-lime, bio-insulation) | None of scale — regional makers, mostly private |
| Plant-based regional food supply chains | Thin and troubled — the listed names are processors far from the soil |
| Sanctuary networks and transitional care | Effectively none — the work is nonprofit and municipal |
| Traditional-urbanism placemaking | No direct vehicle — proxies only, through transit and materials |
| Transparent public-ledger civic finance | None — this is precisely Linengrow's claim |

The market is thick exactly where the machines are — wires, pipes, pavement — and thin exactly
where the living systems are. That is not a verdict against the living chapters; it is a map of
where public investment has not yet been given rails a community can hold. Every empty row in
that table is an argument for building the receipt, the open ledger, the procurement scorecard,
and the demand map ourselves — the strongest conclusion this research supports, and the one that
costs nothing to act on, because it is already on our ladder.

## Part Five — Reading the Whole Board

Braid the five lenses and a quiet ordering emerges without becoming advice. Water and grid
crafts pair the firmest authorizations with wages-as-the-product, and their risk is valuation
and execution rather than the appropriation itself. The design consultancies convert public
intent into built work with almost pure labor, and their risk is the parable — a client can be
signed away. Care is the truest circulation in the set — the mark lands as a wage in a
neighbor's home the same week — and it stands nearest the bending rulebook. Retrofit-by-savings
is the best-designed reward geometry on the board, currently sailing into an administrative
headwind. The bus is the weathervane for administered money. Wind-and-solar development crosses
its cliff tomorrow, while storage, firm-clean, and the grid that carries all of it keep their
footing into the 2030s. And the chapters we love most are not on the board at all — they are on
our workbench.

document exists to understand, not to instruct a trade; Claude is no licensed adviser; figures
