# Systemantics / The Systems Bible — Key Principles (curated)

**Author:** John Gall
**Works:** *General Systemantics* (1975) → *Systemantics* (1986) → *The Systems Bible* (2002)

**A note on this file.** Unlike the talk transcripts kept whole in this folder, Gall's work is a *copyrighted book*, with no legitimate full text online. So this is a **curated record of its key, widely-quoted principles**, gathered from public summaries, rather than the full work. We honor it by keeping its load-bearing ideas close — and by pointing plainly to where the whole book may be bought and read, because it rewards reading entire.

---

## Gall's Law — the keystone

> A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked. A complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be patched up to make it work. You have to start over, beginning with a working simple system.

This is the principle we lean on hardest: complexity is *grown* from working simplicity, never designed whole from nothing.

## The book's primary precept

Large complex systems are extremely difficult to design correctly, despite the best intentions. So care is taken to design *smaller, less-complex* systems, and to grow their function *incrementally* — in close and continual touch with real needs and honest measures of whether the thing actually works.

## Other well-known axioms (in brief)

- **The Primal Scenario:** systems in general work poorly, or not at all — failure to perform is an intrinsic feature of complex systems, not a single hidden defect waiting to be removed.
- **A complex system cannot be *made* to work** — it either works or it does not; a "good swift kick" will not bring to life a system that is not already alive.
- **Systems tend to grow, and as they grow, they encroach.**
- **The Generalized Uncertainty Principle:** systems display antics — they behave in ways no one quite predicted.
- **New systems mean new problems** (the Fundamental Theorem).
- **Complex systems tend to oppose their own proper function** (Le Chatelier, borrowed).
- **Loose systems last longer and work better** — gentle decoupling outlives tight binding.
- **Large complex systems are beyond the full grasp of any single mind.**
- **A system set against human motivation works poorly or not at all.**

(The phrasings above are short, widely-circulated summaries; the book states each with far more wit and depth.)

## How it shapes our work

Gall's Law is the temper beneath our whole method: we grow each module from the smallest version that runs, by proven degrees, rather than designing the finished, intricate whole and hoping it breathes.
