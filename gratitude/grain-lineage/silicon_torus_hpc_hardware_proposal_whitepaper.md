## 2. Solid Silicon Torus Architecture

### 2.1 Toroidal Geometry Fundamentals

Before we dive into the hardware details, let's establish what we mean by a torus and why its geometry matters for field-theoretic computation:

**Torus Definition**: A torus (T²) is the surface of revolution generated by revolving a circle around an axis in its plane. A solid torus (T² × I) includes the interior volume. For our architecture, we use a solid silicon torus—a three-dimensional toroidal volume of silicon.

**Key Geometric Properties**:
- **Compactness**: A torus is compact (bounded and closed), which provides natural constraints for field interactions
- **Periodicity**: Toroidal topology provides periodic boundary conditions—paths that wrap around the torus return to their starting point
- **No Edges**: Unlike flat wafers, a torus has no boundaries—every point has the same topological neighborhood
- **Three-Dimensional Structure**: The toroidal volume provides three-dimensional field interaction space

**Why This Matters for Field Interactions**:

In the aether-theoretic framework, field interactions occur in a continuous medium. The geometry of the medium affects how fields interact. Toroidal geometry provides:

**Structural Dimensions**:

The torus dimensions are optimized for field interaction efficiency and manufacturing feasibility:

- **Major Radius (R)**: The distance from the center of the torus to the center of the tube (outer radius)

- **Volume**: V = 2π²Rr² (compact volume for efficient field interactions)

**Design Optimization**:
