## Why We Read Them Here

These sources name the shape of the **Rhyz** identity vane — the R-vane that revives Kumara. An identity system on a peer network is not a tree with an authority at its root; it is a mesh where any holder can reach any other, entered from anywhere, surviving the loss of any single node. That is a rhizome, plainly. And what such a mesh actually spends — what a person running their own node truly budgets — is not abstract time but *timenergy*: the bounded overlap of hours and capacity. Reading these lets us name Rhyz's shape and its cost honestly, in our own voice.

## The Transferable Insights (siloed)

- **A rooted tree and a rhizome are different network shapes, and identity wants the second.** A tree has one trunk; cut it and everything above dies; every node's meaning flows from its position beneath the root. A rhizome has no privileged center: any point connects to any other, the map can be entered at any node, and a break anywhere heals by re-linking rather than collapsing. For identity on a peer network, the rhizome is the honest shape — no central authority whose failure ends everyone, every holder reachable from every other.
- **A hierarchy of *addressing* can coexist with a rhizome of *connection*.** This is the subtle, load-bearing insight. A tiered address space (a small set of anchors, a larger set of nodes beneath them, a vast set of leaves) is a *naming* convenience — it makes an address short and its sponsor legible. It need not make the *connectivity* hierarchical: two leaves can still speak directly, peer to peer, without routing through their anchors. So a system can wear a tiered name and still connect rhizomatically. Naming this tension plainly keeps us from mistaking the address tree for the connection graph.
- **Entry from anywhere, break without death.** A rhizomatic identity mesh should be enterable at any node (a new holder joins by linking to any existing one, not by petitioning a center) and should survive any node's loss (no single failure orphans the rest). This is a resilience property, not a metaphor.
- **Timenergy is the real budget, and bounded systems respect it.** The scarce resource a self-hosting person spends is the overlap of time and energy. A system that demands constant tending burns timenergy; one that is bounded, quiet, and forgetful of what it need not keep returns it. This is why TAME's bounds and our "device that forgets" foundation are not only safety properties — they are kindnesses to the holder's timenergy. A rhizome that is cheap to stay connected to is one a real person can actually keep.

## What We Keep, and What We Set Down

- **Keep:** the rhizome as the connection shape of identity (no central root, any-to-any, entry-anywhere, break-without-death); the address-tree-vs-connection-graph distinction; timenergy as the honest budget a bounded system respects.
- **Set down:** the wider philosophical and political freight of all three sources. We take the network-shape and the resource-budget insights, not the metaphysics or the ideology. Rhyz is an engineering seam for identity, described plainly; the sources lit the shape, and are thanked here, apart.

## How It Grounds Rhyz

Rhyz — the R-vane identity, reviving Kumara — is a rhizome wearing an Azimuth-style tiered *name*. Its connectivity is any-holder-to-any-holder (the Ames/peer shape); its addressing adopts the point/planet/star tiers Keaton chose, for short legible names with legible sponsors. The two are held distinct on purpose, exactly as the second insight above warns. And its whole discipline — bounded, quiet, forgetful of what it need not keep — is timenergy returned to the person who holds the key.
