## The Invariants, Stated Before Beading

Beading exists to carry a resin too large for one frame, so its invariants come first. A resin divides into content-addressed beads, and a bead-index names their digests in order, so the beads and their arrangement are both named by content. The name a reader holds for a beaded resin is the resin's own content digest, identical to the name it would carry unbeaded, so beading stays an interior representation a reader never has to know about. Recall of a beaded resin returns the very same bytes as the resin unbeaded. Every bead proves itself against its digest as it is gathered, and the reassembled whole proves itself against the resin's recorded digest before a single byte is trusted. A missing bead, a tampered bead, a tampered index, a resin that is not a bead-index, and a bead count past the bound each refuse the whole reassembly. A bead deposited twice is stored once, so beading that shares a bead with another resin moves that bead's bytes not at all. Everything stays bounded by a fixed bead count, a fixed bead budget, and a fixed store.

## What Was Built — Three Rings

The lap grew in three rings, each a working whole the next was grown from, and all sharing one `mantra/beading.rye`.

The first ring is fixed-size beading. `bead` splits a resin into fixed-size beads, deposits each in a content-addressed `BeadStore`, and writes a bead-index whose bytes are the whole digest, the length, and every bead digest in order. `reassemble` reverses it in the disciplined order: the seated `bead-index` Tilak gates the layout, each bead proves itself against its named digest, the running total stays bounded by the promised length, and the reassembled whole proves itself against the recorded digest before it returns. This ring closes the whole beading-and-reassembly loop, and it already earns dedup where beads align.

The second ring is content-defined beading. `nextBeadEnd` finds a boundary where a gear hash over recent bytes meets a mask, honoring a minimum and a maximum bead size, so a boundary follows the content rather than a fixed offset. `beadContentDefined` walks a resin by those boundaries and writes the very same bead-index the first ring writes, so reassembly is shared and unchanged. The gear-hash form comes from FastCDC, and content-defined chunking for content-addressed stores comes from casync; both are named where the technique is used, while the owned vocabulary — bead, beading, the bead-index Tilak — carries the design. No new name was minted; content-defined boundaries are a way of beading, not a new word.

The third ring adds a shared gate and a proof. `parseIndexHeader` gathers the bead-index checks in one place, and both reassembly and `verifyBeadIndex` lean on it. `verifyBeadIndex` confirms a beaded resin is whole and sound using one-bead memory: it fetches each bead, proves it against its named digest, folds its bytes into a running whole hash, and proves that hash against the recorded whole digest, holding no output buffer, so a resin of any size verifies in the space of a single bead. Alongside it, the selftest proves the name invariance directly: for one resin, both rings return a whole digest identical to the unbeaded content digest and to each other, so a reader who knows a resin by its content address gets the same name whether the resin rests whole or beaded.

## Verification on This Bench

`beading.rye` builds clean against the pinned Zig 0.16 toolchain and stock `std`, importing only `std`; the beading selftest greens across all three rings and every welcome and unwelcome assertion. A 400-byte resin beads into two fixed-size beads and reassembles to the byte, and a second fixed resin sharing a bead deposits only its one new bead. A 480-byte resin beads into six content-defined beads; the same content with three bytes inserted near the front re-syncs five of its six beads, and the same content with three bytes deleted re-syncs five as well, where fixed-size beading re-syncs none across either edit. Bounded-memory verify confirms the whole in one-bead space and agrees with reassembly, and a tampered index fails verify exactly as it fails reassembly. The refusals all land whole: a missing bead, a corrupted bead, a flipped byte in the recorded whole digest, a non-bead-index Tilak, and a bead size small enough to exceed the bead bound.

The resin-batch frame reran green beside the module, and the namespace laps NS-L1 through NS-L3 and the wire laps stay green, because beading adds a file rather than editing one — accrete-never-break holds in fact. The file carries zero `usize` literals, no tabs, and every line within one hundred bytes. Because the work lives in `mantra/beading.rye`, which this bundle places in the width-check roster and the parity suite through one beading witness, no further witness or roster line is needed; the single witness now exercises all three rings.

The full parity suite is Cursor's to run on metal. This bench verified the beading witness in isolation and rebuilt the shipped file to confirm it matches the file that greened. The suite gains one witness over parity 163; its total is pinned on metal rather than predicted here.
