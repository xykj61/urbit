## The Lens We Read By

We hold a distinction, plainly: **simple and easy are different things, measured on different axes.**

*Simple* is about how a thing is made — whether its parts stand free, each about one thing (single-stranded, of one fold), or whether they are braided through one another. *Easy* is about how near a thing lies — how familiar it feels, how quickly we can reach for it, how well it fits the skills already in hand. A thing can be easy and tangled, or simple and unfamiliar; one rarely decides the other.

We build by **composing**: laying free parts side by side, so any one can be drawn out, understood, and changed on its own. We choose the simple over the merely easy, even when the easy lies closer to hand, because a person holds only a few things in mind at once — so parts that stand free stay within reach of understanding, while braided ones soon pass beyond it. With that lens in hand, we read what we have built.

## Reading the Architecture for What Stands Free

**Each module is about one thing.** Rye is the programs; Brix is the composing language (what a system is made of); Tablecloth is the database (application data, structured and queryable); Brix has its own build store; Rishi is the human's hand at the prompt; Mantra is the memory; Tally is the bounded living space; Caravan is the isolation and supervision; Pond is one agent's enclosure. Each is single-stranded — about one thing, free to be understood and changed on its own. The easy path would have been one tool doing all of it, familiar and near; the simple path keeps each free, and we take it.

**They compose through one value model.** The parts meet by sharing one model of values, so a value passes from one to the next without being flattened to text and reparsed. That shared model *is* composition without braiding: free parts set side by side, each still its own. The seam we refuse — the place where a value must be marshaled across a boundary — is exactly the braid we decline to tie. No seam is no braid.

**The enclosure is a value, not braided flags.** A sandbox policy that stands free — a value naming what may be seen, what may be changed, what bounds hold — composes side by side with everything else, and can be read, asserted, and remembered on its own. The familiar, easy way scatters the same intent across stringly flags, braiding meaning into syntax. We choose the value.

**Pond composes two free primitives.** It does not braid isolation and bounding into one tangled thing. It composes Caravan's isolation, which is about one thing, with Tally's bounded gardens, which are about one thing — two single-stranded primitives, set side by side, making a wall. Each can be reasoned about, hardened, and trusted apart from the other.

**The gates are single-stranded assurances.** Behavior, shape, and the gate's own trustworthiness are three separate questions, each guarded by its own check, composed — rather than one tangled "is this alright" that no one could fully reason about. Each gate is about one thing, so each can be believed.

**The strengthening keeps things simple across time.** Each pass states one invariant, and accretion lays each new statement beside the old ones rather than re-weaving the whole — composition across time, not a re-braiding. The history stays single-stranded too: each change is a thread you can still draw out and read alone.

## The Choice, Made Again and Again

Simplicity is not a state we reach and keep; it is a choice offered at every turn, with the easy path standing close by each time. One language for everything would be familiar. Stringly flags would be quick. A grand rewrite would feel decisive. Each is easy, and each braids. So we choose, again and again, the single-stranded way — one module per thing, one value model, a policy that is a value, an accretion rather than a rewrite — and we pay simplicity its due in care and a steady eye. It is the hardest revision rather than the first attempt, and it is worth every pass of thought it asks.

---

## The Reward

What we gain is the thing every builder longs for: the freedom to change course. Because each part stands free and can be reasoned about, we can swap one language without disturbing the others, re-shape an enclosure's policy without unweaving the kernel, strengthen one function without fear for the rest. Reliability follows from the same root — a fault has fewer places to hide when each part is about one thing — and so does understanding, which is simply the mind keeping pace with a design that never asks it to hold too much at once. Single-stranded, composed, and free: that is how a system stays both safe and changeable, year upon year.

---

## A Caveat We Keep in View

The easy path never leaves; it returns at every decision, wearing the face of speed or familiarity. Keeping the design simple asks for vigilance we must renew rather than spend once. We write this down precisely so the choice stays conscious — so that when the easy way beckons, we recognize it, weigh it honestly, and choose the free-standing way on purpose.
