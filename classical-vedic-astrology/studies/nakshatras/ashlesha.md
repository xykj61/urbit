## The star of the serpent

Ashleṣā is the nakshatra of the Nāgas, the divine serpents — the dragons of the deep — and its symbol is the coiled snake. To meet this star is to meet a paradox held in a single coil: an immense power to embrace, and the choice of what to do with that embrace.

The word Ashleṣā means intimate contact — entwining, adhering, clinging, holding close. A serpent wraps itself around what it loves. So this star gives the capacity to love with depth and endurance, to hold a person or a purpose with one's whole length, to carry great responsibility for the sake of those one loves. It gives charisma, charm, a certain mystique — a magnetism others feel before they can name it. These are real gifts, and they are Alice's.

## The two coils: which way one winds

The Nāgas themselves tell the rest. In the old story their mother carried jealousy and cunning, and she asked her serpent children to help her deceive. Some obeyed. Yet some refused — they would not lie, even for their mother — and those moral serpents became a separate, luminous branch of the Nāga race, keepers of true spirituality and deep mysticism.

This is the heart of Ashleṣā. The same star that can coil toward jealousy, secrecy, and clever self-protection can also coil toward devotion, loyalty, and the sacred. The lower coil and the higher coil run through one body, and the whole life-lesson is which way one winds.

## The serpent's highest form

The Bhāgavata Purāṇa gives the serpent its highest form. Ananta-Śeṣa, the endless serpent, holds the worlds upon his hoods and forms the resting-couch of the Lord — strength made entirely into service and love. Kāliya, the proud and venomous serpent, is not destroyed by Kṛṣṇa but danced upon until he surrenders, and then sent away blessed. And the gopīs, the cowherd maidens, embody this star's energy in its perfection: by the sheer force of their love they keep the Supreme himself wrapped close — not to possess him for themselves, but because their embrace is pure devotion. This is the star's true north. The serpent's power is real; in the hands of love it becomes the couch of God.

## Charm without karma

There is a teaching here, too, about charm and influence, for this star knows how to draw others in. The art is to draw them toward their own joy, rather than toward one's own gain — to lead a person, gently, into what makes them happy and whole, and to want nothing back. Held that way, magnetism carries no shadow; it becomes a kind of care. Held the other way, it tightens into manipulation, and the serpent bites its own tail.

## Holding this gently, in the student season

A word of warmth for where Alice stands. The passion in this star is genuine and strong. She need not spend it loosely, nor fear it. Held with temperance, allowed to ripen, the embrace can aim upward — toward the Divine, toward true friends, toward a love worthy of so much loyalty. Ahiṃsā is the serpent's quiet vow: the power to hold, never to harm; to shelter, never to poison.
