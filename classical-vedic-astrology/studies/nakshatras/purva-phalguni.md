## The meaning in the name

The word *Phalguṇī* carries its whole teaching. *Phala* means fruit — the result of an action. *Guṇa* means the perceivable quality of a thing. Put together, Phalguṇī names the perceivable part of the fruit: the peel, the skin, the shell — the part that holds the fruit, carries it, and brings it forth into the world. So this is the star of the **fruit-bearer**: the bringer-into-being, the one who gives things form.

That makes Pūrva Phalguṇī a deeply **creative** star. Fruit means fertile, productive, generative. And because it governs the perceivable surface of things, it gives a real gift for *form* — for shape, for the sensual and visible expression of an idea. This is one reason the star serves art so well, since art is exactly the act of bringing something mysterious and inward into a concrete, perceivable, beautiful form. A planet here knows how to clothe an idea in a shape that can be seen and felt.

## A star that comes in a pair

Pūrva Phalguṇī is one half of a pair — the first star-pair we meet, two neighboring stars sharing a name and a symbol. *Pūrva* means the first, the initial stage, the way you approach and lead into a thing. *Uttara* means the next and the higher, the full flowering of the same thing. The stem and then the flower; the lead-in and then the full manifestation.

Both halves share the symbol of the bed — the bed of love, marriage, and union. So both are stars of affection and partnership. Pūrva Phalguṇī holds the *first* experience: the spark, the falling-in-love, the thrill and pleasure of attraction, the bright beginning. Uttara Phalguṇī holds the *mature* experience: the long bond, the marriage, the keeping of what was begun.

There is a strength to each. Pūrva is *ugra* — fierce, brave, full of initiative, the energy that *starts* a thing. Uttara is *dhruva* — fixed, patient, enduring, the strength that *holds* a thing together. One lights the spark; the other keeps the fire.

## The Phalguṇī switcheroo, and why we lead with Aryaman

Here is a subtlety worth naming plainly, because it shapes how we read Alice's chart. Two old and trustworthy sources assign the gods of these stars differently. The older ritual tradition places Bhaga — the god of pleasure and treasured things — in Pūrva, and Aryaman — the god of promises and devotion — in Uttara. The nakshatra teaching we follow reverses this, placing Aryaman in Pūrva and Bhaga in Uttara. This is no error; both orderings are deliberate.

The practical resolution is elegant: for matters of ritual and timing, follow the older tradition; for *interpretation* — for reading what a placement means in a life — take Aryaman's qualities into Pūrva Phalguṇī, and Bhaga's into Uttara. So for Alice's Mercury and Jupiter here, we lead with **Aryaman**. This is the teaching we most want to draw out anyway, because it offers the truest guidance for a young person building toward devoted partnership.

## Aryaman — promises, fidelity, and friendship as the ground of love

Aryaman is the god of promises and vows, of friendship and devotion. His very name grows from a root meaning *friendly, true, trustworthy, reliable, dedicated* — the coming-together of people into a unit founded on trust. He is invoked in every wedding, because marriage is a society's most significant promise. The deepest moral principle of this whole tradition lives in him: keep your promises, and make only the promises you can keep. Kept promises are what cooperation, friendship, and family are built upon.

And here is the heart of his teaching, the one most worth carrying. Trust comes *first*. Before pleasure, before the treasures of love, there must be the ground of trust — the felt certainty that *we are on the same team, you can rely on me, I will do what I said*. We open ourselves only to those we trust. So commitment is not the dull price of love; commitment is the very soil in which love's depth and delight can grow. Friendship is built on service — holding another's good as equal to your own — and service is built on a gentle selflessness. From that foundation, everything beautiful becomes possible.

This is why the tradition honors Aryaman as the most trusted of guides, the one whose promise we are willing to follow even through the hardest passages. His affection is praised as the supreme happiness — so supreme that it brings sweetness even into difficulty, the way two people can smile together even in a hard hour. Affection, the old verse says, makes every path walkable and smooths every obstacle. The secret of an enduring bond is not how smooth the road begins, but how much affection two people bring to walking it together. And affection, finally, is named the essence of beauty itself — for love is what makes a thing beautiful.

So the lesson Alice's Mercury and Jupiter carry, sitting here, is a beautiful and steadying one: **fidelity, truthfulness, devotion, and patience** — the keeping of one's word, the building of trust before depth, the friendship that underlies all lasting love. Her mind and her wisdom are seated in the star of the promise kept.

## Bhaga and the fruition it leads toward

The pair's other half names what all that trust grows *toward*. Bhaga is the god of treasured, prized, delightful things — the wish-fulfilling abundance of love, the pleasure and the flowering. Where Aryaman is the foundation of trust, Bhaga is the harvest of joy that the foundation makes safe. Uttara Phalguṇī, his star, speaks of the long, settled happiness of devoted partnership — the felicity that radiates outward and gladdens a whole household. The two belong together and are never truly separate: the commitment of love and the pleasure of love, each incomplete without the other.

## Alice's Mercury and Jupiter in Pūrva Phalguṇī

It gives her a genuinely creative mind and a gift for bringing ideas into beautiful, perceivable form — a maker's intelligence. She is built to *lead into* connection — to spark it, to give it form — and to do so on the honest foundation that lets it last.

## The classical note

Varāhamihira's old encyclopedia, describing those born under the lunar day of Pūrva Phalguṇī, paints a portrait full of delight: a love of dance, of music, of painting and sculpture, of trade — and a heart that lives in the bright vigor of youth. Across the centuries the picture rhymes with everything above: artistry, the gift for form, and a warm, youthful, creative joy. (*Bṛhat Saṃhitā*, Ch. 15, v. 9, distilled in our own words, with its era-bound phrasing gently set aside.)
