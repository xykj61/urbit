## Why this comes first

A chart reads best inside a frame. Before we look at any one planet, we set down the shape of a whole life, so that every placement finds its proper season. This frame holds two old maps laid over each other: four goals worth living for, and four stages across which we live them. Together they tell us what a young chart is *for* right now, and what it grows toward.

## The four goals

A full life pursues four things, and the order matters.

**Purpose comes first.** Before wealth, before pleasure, before freedom, a life needs a moral compass and a sense of why. This is *dharma* — integrity and direction. Everything sound is built on it; skip it, and the rest has nothing to stand on.

**Then we build.** Once we know our why, the world asks us to make something durable — support, stability, the pillars that hold a family and a community. This is *artha*. We raise the pillars, we maintain them, and in time we extend them outward so others can lean on them too.

**Then we enjoy.** Here is a teaching worth holding close: in this tradition, the highest reality's own nature is joy. So the art of delight is not a distraction from the spiritual path; it is part of it. This is *kama* — the craft of joy, affection, beauty, and connection. We learn to bring joy to ourselves, to bring it to others, and to share it widely.

**Then we release.** Only after a life well lived does true freedom ripen. *Moksha* — liberation — comes last, and it cannot be rushed or used as an escape. A person who has built nothing and loved no one has little to offer up. The one who renounces from the fullness of a life that mattered renounces something real.

These four move in sequence, like primary school into secondary into university. You arrive at the freedom of the last by walking honestly through the first three.

## The four stages

The same arc shapes the seasons of our years. Tradition marks four:

The **student** season — *brahmacharya* — devoted to learning, discipline, and the gathering of skill and character.

The **householder** season — *grihastha* — devoted to building a career, a home, a family, and to the full enjoyment of life.

The **forest-dweller** season — *vanaprastha* — devoted to gradual withdrawal, to mentoring, and to service freely given.

The **renunciate** season — *sannyasa* — devoted to liberation and the open sky.

One common reckoning gives each season about twenty-five years. Another, gentler to the body of a real human life, gives each the length of one return of Saturn — close to twenty-nine and a half years, the time it takes that slow teacher to circle back to where it stood at birth. Either way, the seasons unfold in turn, and each prepares the next.

## Where Alice stands

Alice is twenty-one. By both reckonings, she lives squarely in the **student season** — and will for a few years yet, whether we mark the threshold at twenty-five or wait for her first complete Saturn return near twenty-nine. This is the season of *brahmacharya*: learning, gathering capability, choosing teachers well, and tending character with a light and steady discipline.

Her chart leans into the air — quick, curious, communicative, drawn to exchange and ideas. Her Venus in Gemini, which we explore in its own file, is the bright center of this. So *kama*, the art of joy and connection, sits very naturally as the worthy aim of these years for her. She is built to learn through conversation, delight, friendship, and play. That is a gift, and it deserves to be enjoyed.

The student season simply asks that she enjoy it with **temperance** — sobriety rather than excess, light fun rather than heaviness, wisdom carried alongside the play. The joy stays bright precisely because it stays measured. And it deepens fastest under a **qualified, disciplined teacher** — someone who has walked the road and lives by clear principles, so the guidance is embodied rather than merely spoken.

## The kind of teacher worth choosing

A worthy guide for these years lives the teaching, on and off the mat. One clear, beautiful set of principles to look for comes from the path of *jivamukti* — "liberation while living" — which rests on five tenets a true teacher embodies:

- **Ahimsa** — a compassionate, non-violent life that extends to all beings, expressed plainly as veganism and care for the earth. This stands first, the heart that informs the other four.
- **Bhakti** — devotion: offering one's practice to something higher than the small self.
- **Dhyana** — meditation: the steady practice of stillness, watching the mind and resting in the unchanging quiet within.
- **Nada** — sacred sound: deep listening, chanting, and music, cultivating a sound body and a sound mind.
- **Shastra** — study: the careful reading of the foundational texts and the language they were written in.

A teacher who lives these will hold a young student's curiosity and joy without dimming them, and will steady them with discipline rather than fear. Under such guidance, the student season becomes what it is meant to be: a bright, well-tended foundation that lets every season after it stand strong.
