The diurnal wheel places its angles beautifully; this addendum records Kaeden's refinement of one step. The silo method sets each angle at the center of its angular house, which is exactly right when the ascendant stands mid-sign. When it stands elsewhere in its sign, the horizon should cross houses one and seven at the **true zodiac degree** — a Capricorn ascendant of 24°37′ crosses its house four-fifths of the way through, with four-fifths of the rising sign already in the day above the line and one-fifth still in the night below.

The construction that honors this is one rule: for any zodiac degree λ, let u = (ASC − λ) mod 360 be how long ago that degree rose. Risen degrees (u ≤ 180) map from the ascendant across the day arc at dayArc/180 per degree; unrisen degrees map from the descendant across the night arc at nightArc/180 per degree. Every promise of the silo method survives whole — the Midheaven at 90°, midnight at 270°, the level horizon at its true height, day houses wide and night houses narrow — and each cusp, each planet, and each axis now falls at the screen position its own degree earns. When the ascendant is mid-sign, the rule reduces exactly to the silo's half-split, so the two documents describe one method at two levels of generality. The horizontal line, read left to right, is the horizon itself; the whole dial reads as the day.
