# Money That Stays Close to Home

*A currency can be built to keep value in the place that made it, or to drain it away. This piece distills a study of sovereign money and complementary local currency onto our own roots — a national currency pointed at the public good, and a smaller currency beside it that circulates close to home, lawful and complementary, designed to return more each time it moves.*

## Two Kinds of Money

Money is a tool, and like any tool it carries the intentions of whoever shaped it. One kind is the national currency, issued by an authority that brings it into being. Such an authority is held back less by accounting than by what is real — the workers, the materials, the capacity already at hand. When people stand ready and good work waits to be done, that work can be funded and done. Public money, used this way, points toward the public good. The binding limit, in this view, is what is real — and it shows itself as rising prices once idle capacity is fully at work; mainstream economics names that tension plainly, and both views earn their place in honest debate.

Beside the national currency, a community can keep a smaller currency of its own: a local certificate, backed by the labor and the abundance of the place, that circulates among neighbors. They are companions rather than rivals. The large one carries the weight of a whole economy; the small one keeps a community's own value turning close to home.

## A Voucher Backed by Work

The local currency is, at heart, a record of community labor and shared abundance. It coordinates the exchange of work, the lending of tools, the trading of skills, and the quiet generosity of a gift economy, so neighbors can help one another without waiting on distant systems. It can be printed on lasting paper, recorded on a shared ledger, or both — a token plain enough that anyone can hold it and trust it.

What it does best is keep wealth where it was made. Each time the local currency changes hands, its value stays in the community rather than draining away to a far headquarters. A place that circulates its own currency grows more resilient with every exchange, the way good soil holds more water each season.

## Lawful, and Complementary

A local currency of this kind supplements the national one rather than replacing it. It lives in the long, lawful tradition of community scrip and mutual exchange — a private arrangement among neighbors, never a claim to be legal tender, and never a rival to the national currency's stewardship. Taxes are still paid in the national currency, and the token stays within the bounds and the law of the community that issues it. Designed with that care, the small currency strengthens the foundation it rests on rather than straining it.

## Circulation, Not Extraction

The whole point of money shaped this way is that it moves, and that moving it leaves something behind. The cooperative spends it at the market, the grower earns it, the maker is paid by the grower, and the community keeps it turning — a current that feeds every field it passes rather than a pipe that carries the water off. This is the opposite of extraction, where each transaction thins a place a little more. Money built to circulate returns more each time it moves.

## How This Joins the Rest

This is the flow of value the larger vision already names: a sovereign currency pointed at the public good, and a currency beside it that keeps value circulating close to home. The local token can settle on the same decentralized ledger the rest of the work already uses, with the same stable unit as its steady measure, and with every transaction signed, recorded in an append-only record, and checkable by anyone — the same transparent spine that runs beneath the whole system, where state lives as a pure fold over a log of signed facts.

It keeps the four promises we ask of any tool. It does no harm, taking nothing from the people it serves. It tells the truth, in records anyone can read. It holds lightly, leaving the door open for anyone to enter or to leave. And it stays kind, built for the resilience of a whole community rather than the gain of a few. In this it serves the same end as the open commons and the regenerative land: value held close, shared freely, and returning, season after season, more than it ever takes.

## The Giving Lane — Private Payer, Transparent Payee

*Seam study 4 T0 · seated `20260712.054650` · study [`../external-research/20260712-054521_taler-giving-lane-asymmetry.md`](../external-research/20260712-054521_taler-giving-lane-asymmetry.md)*

When a person funds a public or civic good — a sanctuary, an open-ask stipend, a regenerative commons — the till has two faces, and they keep different jobs:

- **Payer face (default privacy).** The giver’s identity and exact amount stay sealed or tier-proved; selective disclosure (SLC-L4 and its kin) and consent glass already begin this work.
- **Payee face (default transparency).** The receiving steward’s *income from the gift* stays auditable on the fold — receipts, books, civic sight with consent rules — so the commons can trust the light without doxing the giver.

MALA and WOV remain the settlement and witness seat. Libre payment teachers (GNU Taler among them) illuminate the asymmetry; they do not replace our log. One job per side of the till.
